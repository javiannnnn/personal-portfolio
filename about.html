About

How I think about problems

I am a second-year Applied AI and Analytics student at Nanyang Polytechnic in Singapore. I build machine learning pipelines, NLP systems, and data-driven applications.

My approach to engineering starts with understanding the problem before reaching for tools. The right model for a task depends on the data, the constraints, and what failure looks like — not on what is popular. I have used gradient boosting when a neural network would overfit on limited data, and I have used deep learning when the feature space was too complex for manual engineering. The decision should follow from the problem.

I care about trade-offs. Every design choice has costs. Choosing BERTopic over LDA gives better topic coherence but requires more compute and is harder to debug. Using walk-forward validation instead of k-fold reduces your test set size but gives realistic performance estimates. I try to make these trade-offs explicit in my work because they are where the real engineering happens.

I am most interested in the intersection of machine learning and quantitative finance — specifically in how reinforcement learning, time series modeling, and alternative data sources can be combined to build systems that make better decisions under uncertainty.

Outside of technical work, I think clearly about what I do not know. The hardest part of any ML project is not the code — it is knowing when your model is wrong and having the discipline to test that assumption rather than shipping something that looks good on paper.
