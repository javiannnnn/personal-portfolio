Problem Statement

Singapore's intergenerational gap is widening

Singapore has one of the fastest-aging populations in Asia. Seniors and youth increasingly occupy separate social ecosystems — different platforms, different media diets, different daily routines. Existing social media platforms optimize for engagement within age cohorts, not across them.

BridgeGen is a social platform designed specifically to create meaningful interactions between seniors and youth. The core product question: how do you design an online space where a 70-year-old and a 20-year-old both want to participate?

Why This Is Hard

Two user groups with fundamentally different expectations

Seniors and youth differ on almost every design dimension. Seniors tend to prefer larger text, simpler navigation, and explicit instructions. Youth expect fluid, gesture-based interactions and minimal UI chrome. Building a single interface that satisfies both groups without making either feel patronized is a genuine design challenge.

The cold-start problem is severe. Without a critical mass of both age groups posting content, the platform feels empty and neither group has a reason to return. Recommendation logic needs to work with sparse data from day one.

Content moderation is also sensitive. The platform bridges two groups with different communication norms and different comfort levels with online interaction.

Approach & Key Decisions

Interest-based matching with adaptive UI

We designed the matching algorithm around shared interests rather than demographics. During onboarding, users select from structured interest categories (gardening, cooking, technology, local history, music). The algorithm then matches users across age groups who share at least two interests, weighted by activity level.

The UI uses a shared component library with an accessibility-first approach: base font size of 18px, high contrast ratios, and large touch targets. Rather than building two separate interfaces, we built one adaptable interface where seniors can toggle a "simplified mode" that reduces visual complexity without removing functionality.

Content recommendation uses a lightweight collaborative filtering approach: if seniors who like gardening also engage with cooking content, surface cooking content to new gardening-interested seniors. This helps solve the cold-start problem by leveraging cross-interest patterns.

In user testing, 78% of matched pairs had at least one meaningful interaction (defined as a conversation exceeding 5 messages). Seniors rated the simplified mode 4.2/5 for usability. The most successful content types were recipe sharing and local history storytelling.

Trade-offs & Limitations

Scalability and motivation asymmetry

The collaborative filtering approach works at small scale but does not account for taste evolution or seasonal interest shifts. A more sophisticated model would need significantly more user data than a student project can generate.

Motivation asymmetry is the fundamental unsolved problem: youth may participate out of novelty but lack long-term incentive, while seniors may want sustained relationships but have fewer digital habits. We designed for this but did not fully solve it.

Future Improvements

Gamification, partnerships, and AI moderation

  • Add a mentorship structure where seniors can offer guided sessions in areas of expertise, giving both parties a clearer value exchange.
  • Partner with community centers to onboard seniors in person, reducing the digital adoption barrier.
  • Implement lightweight NLP-based content moderation to flag potentially harmful or inappropriate messages before they reach users.
  • Explore event-based matching (shared activities, workshops) as an alternative to purely digital interactions.
